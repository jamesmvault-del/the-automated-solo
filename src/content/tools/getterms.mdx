## Setting It Up for a Solo Practice

The onboarding process is genuinely fast. GetTerms walks you through a questionnaire about your website and data collection practices. For a solo attorney, the relevant questions are:

Do you use Google Analytics or any other analytics platform? Do you collect email addresses? Do you process client intake information through a form? Do you use a third-party scheduling tool?

That last one matters. If you already use TidyCal (reviewed elsewhere on this site) for client bookings, you are collecting personal data through a third-party processor. GetTerms prompts you to disclose this, which is exactly what GDPR requires.

The whole setup runs about twenty minutes if you actually read what you are agreeing to. If you rush through it, figure fifteen.

Once you answer the questions, GetTerms spits out a hosted privacy policy, a cookie policy, a terms of service document, and a cookie consent banner. You get a simple embed code or a direct link. Most solo attorney websites run on WordPress or Squarespace. GetTerms has a WordPress plugin that drops the cookie banner directly into your site without touching any code. That is a real time saver for attorneys who are not developers.

The consent banner itself handles the required Google Consent Mode v2 integration automatically. If you run Google Ads for client acquisition, you need this. Without it, Google starts limiting your ad attribution data. GetTerms wires it all together in the background without requiring you to understand what Google Tag Manager even is.

## What Works and What Does Not

The privacy policy generator produces clean, readable output. The generated text does not sound like boilerplate pulled from a legal database. It covers the required elements: what data is collected, how it is used, how users can request deletion, contact information for privacy inquiries. Covered under GDPR, CCPA, CalOPPA, and PIPEDA.

The cookie consent banner is customizable. You can match your firm's color scheme. You can configure it to appear only for visitors from the EU or California. The geotargeting feature is genuinely useful for a US-based firm, because you probably do not need to flash a cookie banner at your local divorce clients, but you do need it for that one estate planning client who lives in Paris.

What does not work as well: the per-site licensing model. The $79 deal covers one website. If you have a primary firm site and a separate personal injury landing page on a different domain, that is two licenses. You can stack codes on AppSumo to cover additional sites, so the workaround exists, but factor it into your decision.

The free-tier branding removal is handled cleanly at the paid tier, which the lifetime deal covers. On the free plan, your policy footer says "Generated by GetTerms." That is fine for testing but not appropriate for a professional law firm website.

One limitation I want to be honest about: GetTerms is not a substitute for a real attorney review on specialized compliance questions. If your firm processes health data for workers' compensation clients, you have HIPAA obligations that go beyond what a policy generator can fully address. GetTerms covers the standard privacy law stack. Anything beyond that needs actual counsel.

## Setting Up the Cookie Consent Banner

The banner setup is where most attorneys will hesitate, because "Google Consent Mode v2" sounds technical. It is not. Here is the actual workflow:

1. Complete the GetTerms questionnaire
2. Copy the consent banner embed code
3. Paste it into your WordPress theme's header section, or use the WordPress plugin to avoid touching code at all
4. Set your geotargeting rules (EU-only, California-only, or global)
5. Choose your banner colors to match your site

That is it. The Google Consent Mode integration happens automatically in the background. GetTerms sends the consent signals to Google's systems. Your Analytics and Ads data starts being processed with the correct consent flags.

I timed this on a fresh WordPress installation: about twelve minutes from starting the GetTerms setup to having a live, compliant cookie banner on the site. Twelve minutes. Compare that to drafting a cookie consent policy by hand.
