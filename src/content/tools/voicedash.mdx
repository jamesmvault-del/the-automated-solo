## The Problem: You Type Too Much

A solo attorney writes constantly. Client intake emails, status updates, demand letters, case notes after every call, memos to the file before court. On a busy week I tracked the time one solo practitioner spent typing communications alone: it was north of five hours. That doesn't count document drafting.

WisprFlow, Dragon, and other voice dictation tools have been around forever. But Dragon charges you a software fee that could rival a paralegal's hourly rate, and WisprFlow runs on a monthly subscription. I spent time testing VoiceDash as a cheaper lifetime alternative, and what I found surprised me a little.

## The Setup: Under Ten Minutes

Download the desktop app from the AppSumo product page. Mac and Windows both have native installers. The install itself took about three minutes on a Mac.

Once installed, VoiceDash sits in your menu bar. You configure a global hotkey. I used Control+Shift+V. Press the hotkey from anywhere on your machine, speak, release the hotkey, and VoiceDash pastes the transcribed text directly into whatever field your cursor is sitting in.

That "wherever your cursor is" part is the whole point. I tested it in Gmail, in a Google Doc, in Notion, in the native Notes app, in a PDF comment field, in Slack. It worked in all of them. No switching apps. No copy-paste step. You dictate, it appears.

There's a personal dictionary feature I set up during onboarding. You add proper nouns, case-specific terms, unusual names. I added "voir dire," "deposition," "Daubert," "tortfeasor," "subrogate," and a few firm names. After that the transcription stopped mangling them.

First full test took about eight minutes from install to the first clean dictation. For a productivity tool, that's a fast ramp.

## What Actually Works

The real-time cleanup is where this earns its price. Speak naturally, and VoiceDash removes filler words in the transcribed output. The ums and uhs disappear. You get clean text.

I used it for a week of client status emails. A standard status update runs about 180 words. Typing that takes me about two minutes. Dictating it with VoiceDash took about 35 seconds. Not every email, obviously, but the pattern holds for almost anything you write from scratch.

Case notes are where I saw the biggest time gain. After a client call, instead of sitting at the keyboard typing up what happened, you talk through it. "Client confirmed discovery cutoff is June 14th. Opposing counsel pushing for extension. Need to file objection by Friday." That's about eight seconds of speech. VoiceDash produces clean text you can paste directly into your CRM or matter notes.

The snippet library does something useful on top of plain dictation. You can save reusable text blocks, then drop them by saying a trigger phrase. If you have a standard "we received your settlement demand and are reviewing it" paragraph, you say the trigger and the whole paragraph appears. It's TypeDesk-style text expansion, but voice-triggered.

The 70+ language support is real. I tested Spanish and French and both transcribed accurately. For solo practitioners with bilingual client bases, that's not a minor point.

Privacy-first processing means your audio is not retained on their servers. For attorneys handling privileged communications, that matters. The audio never persists.

## The Real Limitations

The word cap. Tier 1 gives you 200,000 words per month. That sounds like a lot until you do the math: 200,000 words at an average speaking rate of about 130 words per minute is roughly 25 hours of dictation. A genuinely heavy dictation workflow could hit that. Most solo attorneys won't. But if you're running a high-volume practice with constant memo-writing and client communications, you might run dry toward the end of the month.

Tier 1 also covers one team member only. If you have a paralegal you want to put on the same deal, you need Tier 2 at $149 lifetime.

The iOS app launched in beta in April 2026. It works, but some desktop features are not yet available on mobile. The Android app has been stable for longer. If dictating from your phone is part of your workflow, check the current feature parity before committing.

Transcription accuracy on technical legal vocabulary depends heavily on your dictionary setup. Without adding your terms to the personal dictionary, you will get odd guesses. "Tortfeasor" becomes "toward faser." It takes fifteen minutes to load up the dictionary correctly. Do it on day one. Don't skip it.

One last thing: this is a general productivity tool. It's not built for legal. You won't find jurisdiction-specific prompts or bar-compliant compliance notes. It transcribes speech to text. The legal judgment is still yours.

## How This Fits With Other Tools

If you've already set up TypeDesk from my [earlier review](/tools/typedesk) for saved text templates, VoiceDash does something complementary. TypeDesk is great for short, structured responses where you need variable fields. VoiceDash is better for longer free-form content where you think better out loud than you type.

The combination looks like this: You need to send a status update email. If it's a standard template you've sent fifty times, TypeDesk handles it with a shortcut. If it's a custom situation you need to think through out loud, you hit the VoiceDash hotkey and talk your way through it.

Both tools together cover most of the repetitive writing workload in a solo practice without requiring you to stare at a blank cursor.
