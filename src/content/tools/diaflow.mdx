## The Problem: Your Practice Runs on Repetitive Workflows

Every solo attorney I have watched closely does the same 10 things every week. Drafting a client intake summary from a form. Copying notes into a retainer template. Sending a follow-up email three days after a consultation. Pulling the same boilerplate from six different places to build a demand letter.

None of that requires your law degree. But you are doing it anyway, because nobody built the automation yet.

Diaflow is a no-code AI workflow platform that lets you connect triggers, logic, and AI steps into automated pipelines. It is not a legal practice management tool. Think of it as the layer underneath your tools, the part that moves information between them without you touching a keyboard. For a solo attorney running a lean shop, that distinction matters enormously.

I spent about a week testing Diaflow against the kind of tasks that consume unrecoverable time in a solo practice.

## Setting Up Your First Intake Automation

The fastest win I found was a client intake pipeline. Here is how the build went:

The trigger is a webhook. When a prospect fills out your intake form (Jotform, Tally, or any tool that supports webhooks), Diaflow catches the payload. No code. You paste the webhook URL into your form tool.

From there, the workflow branches. I built a step that passes the intake data to an OpenAI node with a system prompt I wrote in about 15 minutes. The prompt instructs it to produce a one-page intake summary, pull out the key facts, and flag any immediate conflict-check keywords. In my testing, the summary came back in about 8 seconds. Clean, formatted, and dumped directly into a Diaflow Document (their built-in doc layer) with the client's name in the title.

The final step sends an automated email via a connected SMTP node. The prospect gets a confirmation that their intake was received. You get a Slack or email notification with the summary attached.

That entire sequence, from form submission to attorney notification, runs in under 30 seconds. And you never touched it.

Look, does it require 45 minutes of setup? Yes. But that 45 minutes happens once.

If you already use Typedesk for your text snippets, the combination here gets interesting. You build your boilerplate templates in Typedesk. Diaflow handles the dynamic context injection, pulling client-specific details from the intake webhook and feeding them into the right template slot. I tested this workflow twice and it cut the draft turnaround for a standard fee agreement from about 20 minutes to under 3 minutes.

## What Works Well

**The AI integration is genuinely flexible.** You can connect your own API keys for OpenAI, Anthropic, Perplexity, and a handful of others. When you bring your own key, Diaflow does not charge you credits for those steps. For a solo practice running lean, this is how you keep the ongoing cost near zero after the one-time purchase.

**Document OCR is solid.** I uploaded a scanned PDF of a signed client retainer and ran it through the Vision node. Diaflow extracted the text cleanly, including handwritten edits in the margin. Not perfect on dense cursive, but good enough to pull structured data out of printed forms.

**The built-in database (Diaflow Tables) actually replaces a lot of the CRM scaffolding** solo attorneys cobble together in Notion or Airtable. You build a table, link it to your workflows, and now your intake summaries get logged automatically alongside contact info and case status. No monthly Airtable subscription required.

**The template library gets you moving fast.** There are pre-built flows for things like document summarization, lead qualification, and email extraction. None of them are legal-specific, but they are close enough to fork and modify.

## What Does Not Work

The credit model has caused confusion for previous buyers. Diaflow reset credit denominations in early 2026 and rebalanced execution costs. Existing reviews describe it as a 90% cost reduction per execution, which sounds good, but multiple AppSumo users reported that their credit balances looked lower after the change. The vendor explains the math and it does hold up, but the communication was clumsy. You should go in knowing credits are the metered resource, and the Tier 1 deal at $89 will last a long time for a solo practice running a few dozen workflows per day.

**Support response time is slower than I would like.** I sent a question about webhook authentication and waited about 36 hours for a reply. That is manageable for a solo who sets things up once and walks away, but painful during initial build.

**The learning curve is real.** The workflow canvas is intuitive if you have ever used Zapier or Make. If you have not, plan for an afternoon before your first automation is running correctly. The co-pilot feature (which generates a workflow from a text description) helped, but it still required manual cleanup about 60% of the time.

**No native Clio or MyCase integration.** If your practice is built around one of those platforms, you will need to use the HTTP Request node to connect via their APIs. Doable, not elegant.
