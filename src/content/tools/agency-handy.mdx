## What I Actually Set Up

The first thing I built was a service catalog. Agency Handy gives you a public-facing storefront where you can list legal services with fixed prices, package descriptions, and optional add-ons. I configured three entries: a $500 flat-fee LLC formation service, a $1,500 uncontested divorce package, and a $250 initial consultation with a payment required at booking.

That took about 25 minutes, including uploading a logo and setting a custom domain preview.

Next up: client intake. The form builder supports page breaks, custom fields, and section dividers. I built a "New Client Intake" form asking for contact details, matter type, urgency level, and a brief description of the legal issue. Agency Handy's Public Form Automation connects this form directly to the CRM. When someone submits the intake form, a lead record appears automatically. No copy-pasting. No manual entry. If you've read the [automate-client-intake playbook](/playbooks/automate-client-intake) on this site, the same logic applies here, just with a lighter-weight tool.

Payment runs through Stripe. Setup was about 3 minutes: connect the account, set currency to USD, done. Clients can pay by card directly through the checkout flow when they order a service from your catalog. I also tested the standalone invoice builder. You can create an invoice from scratch, attach it to any client record, and send it with a payment link. Agency Handy auto-generates an invoice the moment a proposal is accepted, which removes a step that most solos do manually.

The client portal is white-labeled under your domain. Clients log in and see their active matters (called "orders" inside Agency Handy), review any documents you've shared through the portal, and track their invoice status. I ran a simulated client through the full flow. From their perspective, it looks like a polished branded portal, not a generic SaaS tool.

For a solo attorney who fields "has my invoice been received?" calls at 4 PM on Fridays, that portal alone is worth the $74.

## The Real Limitations

Agency Handy is not a practice management system. It has no trust accounting, no IOLTA compliance tools, no court date tracking, and no matter-specific billing rates. If you need those things, you need Clio, MyCase, or PracticePanther. Full stop. Don't try to force Agency Handy into that role.

Three deal-breaker limits you should know before buying.

**Active leads are capped at 100 on Tier 1.** For most solos handling 20 to 40 active matters at a time, this is plenty of room. If you run a high-volume personal injury intake operation taking 150 consultations per quarter, you'll hit the ceiling. You'd need to archive old leads manually or move up to Tier 2.

**File storage runs at 1 GB per user on Tier 1.** This makes Agency Handy unsuitable as a document management system for case files. Think of it as storage for your intake packets and signed proposals, not your motion practice. Keep actual case documents in Google Drive or a dedicated document management solution.

**There's no native time-based billing.** You can log tasks and track time spent. But there's no way to automatically calculate "3.5 hours at $275/hour" and roll it into an invoice the way Clio does. You calculate the amount manually, then enter it. For hourly billing, this is a friction point. For flat-fee billing, it's completely irrelevant.

The billing issue is the one I hear about most from users who tested it. If you've built your practice around hourly rates and detailed time entries, this is a genuine gap. If you've moved to flat-fee or productized legal services, you'll never notice it's missing.

One more thing worth mentioning: the platform had real bugs in its early AppSumo campaigns. The founder's update from March 2026 documents what they've fixed since then, and the changelog is substantial. In my testing, I didn't hit the invoice calculation bugs or the email template issues that showed up in older reviews. The product has matured.
