## The Problem With Traditional PR for Solo Attorneys

Cision, Muck Rack, Vocus. These are the professional PR databases every major firm uses. The entry price is $5,000 a year at minimum, and the database alone doesn't actually do anything. You still hire a PR consultant to use it. That is $3,000 to $8,000 a month for a full-service engagement. For a solo practice, that number is a fantasy.

On the other end, you have DIY options: emailing journalists you find on LinkedIn, hoping HARO (now Connectively) serves up relevant queries, occasionally submitting a press release to a wire service that charges $400 per distribution and reaches a bunch of sites nobody reads. None of this is strategic. None of it compounds.

Press Ranger sits between those two extremes. It is a database of over 2 million journalists and publishers, paired with AI tools that actually help you write and send pitches, plus a dedicated podcast directory if you want to get on shows instead of in print.

## Setting Up for a Solo Practice

I set the tool up as if I were managing marketing for a general practice solo attorney. The onboarding is fast. Under 20 minutes to profile setup.

**Step 1: Build your firm profile.** You enter your practice areas, geographic focus, and a brief description of your firm. The AI uses this to surface relevant journalists and match your profile when generating pitches. I used "estate planning, family law, probate" for the practice focus and added a geographic filter for a mid-sized metropolitan area.

**Step 2: Run your first journalist search.** I searched for journalists who cover family law, estate planning, and local business. The database returned hundreds of contacts with their beat, publication, recent articles, email addresses, and social handles. You can filter by outlet size, topic, and location. Hyper-local contacts (your city's neighborhood weekly) are sometimes thin, but regional newspapers and mid-size legal trade publications are well-covered.

**Step 3: Set up pitch request alerts.** This is the feature that replaces HARO for me. When journalists post a query looking for expert sources, you get notified if it matches your profile keywords. I set alerts for "estate planning," "probate," "family law attorney," and "small business legal." Within the first 24 hours, I had two relevant queries come in, one from a financial publication asking for attorney commentary on a recent change to estate tax exemption thresholds.

**Step 4: Draft with the AI pitch writer.** I pulled up one of the alerts and clicked generate. The AI pulled context from my firm profile and the journalist's query, then drafted a pitch in about 90 seconds. It was not perfect out of the box. I rewrote the opener and added a specific local statistic. But the structure was sound and it saved the blank-page problem entirely.

**Step 5: Distribute a press release.** I drafted a press release about a hypothetical firm milestone (five years in practice, recognizing the increase in estate planning consultations post-pandemic). The built-in AI helped structure it according to standard press release format. For direct outreach, you send it through the platform. If you want guaranteed placement in outlets like Business Insider or Yahoo Finance, there is a wholesale distribution add-on priced separately at around $399 per premium release.

The podcast database is worth flagging separately. Press Ranger lists over 200,000 podcast contacts in the system (per the AppSumo product listing as of March 2026). For a solo attorney, guest appearances on local business podcasts, financial planning shows, or general legal topics can be more valuable per hour than press releases. I found 14 active, relevant shows in under 10 minutes.

## What Actually Works

The pitch request alerts are the highest-leverage feature for solo attorneys. You are not cold pitching randomly. You are responding to journalists who have explicitly said "I need a legal expert." That conversion rate is dramatically higher than blasting a press release into the void.

The AI writing assistant handles the mechanics well. It knows AP style. It structures a pitch correctly. It pulls relevant context from your profile so you are not rewriting basic bio information every time. I tested about a dozen pitches during my two weeks and the AI drafts saved me 15 to 25 minutes per pitch on average.

The campaign tracking is also genuinely useful. When you send pitches through the platform, you see open rates, click tracking, and reply tracking. Most solo practitioners doing PR by hand have no idea what happened after they hit send.

## What Does Not Work (Honest Gotchas)

**Hyper-local coverage is inconsistent.** I found solid coverage for regional newspapers and major metropolitan areas, but community news sites, neighborhood blogs, and niche local weeklies are hit or miss. If your entire practice geography is a single county in a rural state, the database may feel thin.

**The wholesale press release placement costs extra.** The AppSumo lifetime deal gives you the platform, the database, and unlimited direct outreach. Guaranteed placements in major outlets (Yahoo Finance, AP, etc.) are not included. Each premium release is around $399 a la carte. For a solo attorney who wants one credible "as seen on" logo for their website, this is a one-time cost that makes sense. But do not assume it is part of the deal.

**There is a learning curve for the media angle.** The tool does not write your positioning strategy for you. You still need to decide: what is the angle? Why would a journalist care right now? For attorneys who have never thought about media, the AI helps with the mechanics but the hook has to come from you. This takes a few attempts to calibrate.

**The database gravitates toward US news.** The founders say this directly. If you have an international practice or serve a primarily non-English-speaking client base, the results are less complete.
