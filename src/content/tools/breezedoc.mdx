## The Workflow: Kill the DocuSign Bill

Every solo attorney I've talked to lately has the same complaint. They're paying DocuSign $25 to $45 a month for a tool they use maybe 15 times. That's $300 to $540 a year just to collect signatures on engagement letters and settlement releases. You did the work to win the client. Now you're paying a subscription tax just to get them to sign the paperwork.

I tested BreezeDoc across a simulated solo practice workflow for a full week: engagement letters, retainer agreements, contractor NDAs, and a settlement release. It's an AppSumo Original, which means AppSumo built it themselves, so it's not going to disappear after six months. Here is what I found.

### Getting It Set Up

Account creation takes about three minutes. No credit card required to start. You land in a clean dashboard with a single prominent button: "New Document."

Here's how the actual signing workflow runs for a typical engagement letter:

1. **Upload your document.** Drag in a PDF or Word file. I used a standard flat-fee retainer template. Took about 15 seconds to process.
2. **Place your fields.** Drag a signature block, a date field, and an initials field onto the document. BreezeDoc uses a standard point-and-click field editor. No learning curve. I had my fields positioned in under two minutes.
3. **Add your client.** Type in their name and email address. You can set a signing order if you need countersignature: client signs first, then your block.
4. **Send.** One click. BreezeDoc fires off a branded email to the client. They click the link, sign directly in their browser, and both of you get a PDF copy via email.

That entire process, from uploading to sending, takes about four minutes once you've done it once. Compare that to the ritual humiliation of logging into DocuSign, navigating their pricing-upsell minefield, and fumbling with their ancient "tagging" interface.

### Where It Actually Shines

The audit trail is solid. Every time a recipient opens the document, clicks a field, or signs, BreezeDoc logs it with a timestamp. That matters in a legal context. If a client later claims they never received the engagement letter, you have a documented trail showing the email was delivered, opened, and signed. I've had that exact conversation with attorneys who were burned by undocumented agreements.

Templates save the real time here. I built a retainer agreement template once, saved it, and from that point on, every new client engagement took under 90 seconds to send for signature. You fill in the client name and fee amount, BreezeDoc handles the rest.

The Agency plan ($49 lifetime) now includes invoice sending tied directly to Stripe or PayPal. I tested this. You send the retainer agreement for signature, and once it's signed, you manually create and send the invoice for the retainer deposit directly from the same BreezeDoc dashboard, with the client's information pre-filled. Two separate tools replaced by one. For a solo attorney running a flat-fee family law or estate planning practice, this is genuinely useful.

Zapier and API access are included, which means you can connect BreezeDoc to your intake workflow. If you have already built the zero-touch intake pipeline covered in [The Zero-Touch Client Intake System](/playbooks/automate-client-intake), you can automate the engagement letter step completely: new intake form submission triggers a pre-built BreezeDoc template sent to the client's email with zero manual input.

### What Doesn't Work

No mobile app. BreezeDoc runs in a mobile browser and it's functional, but it's not a dedicated app experience. Clients signing on their phone occasionally run into issues with signature box sizing. I saw this in user reviews and confirmed it in testing. For clients who will sign on desktop, it's fine.

The Individual plan ($19 lifetime) caps you at 20 documents per month. For a busy solo attorney handling multiple practice areas, that limit gets tight fast. A single contested divorce file might generate a dozen documents requiring client signatures. The Agency plan at $49 removes the hard ceiling and allows up to 100 documents per month, which is enough for almost any solo practice.

BreezeDoc does not carry HIPAA certification or the European eIDAS advanced signature standard. They are transparent about this in their product updates. For US-based civil and family law work, the E-Sign Act compliance is what matters, and BreezeDoc was built specifically with the US E-Sign Act in mind. If you are handling matters where HIPAA or eIDAS is a hard requirement, look at a different tool.

There is no built-in document editor. You have to prepare your documents externally and then upload. That is not a dealbreaker, since most solo attorneys are working from Word templates anyway, but it is worth knowing going in.
