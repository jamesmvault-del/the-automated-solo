## My Setup Workflow, End to End

I wanted to test a real flow, not click around for five minutes and call it a review. So I set up NinjaPipe around a common new-matter path: website inquiry, consultation booking, follow-up sequence, and signed engagement.

**Step 1: Build one intake form and map the fields first.**
I started with a short form: name, email, phone, practice area, urgency, and preferred consult time. NinjaPipe Tier 1 includes 10 custom forms, so you can keep separate forms for family law, estate planning, and PI without hacks.

**Step 2: Create a five-stage pipeline before importing contacts.**
I used these stages: New Inquiry, Qualified, Consultation Booked, Proposal Sent, Signed. Tier 1 includes 5 pipelines, which is enough for matter-type separation if you want one pipeline per practice area.

**Step 3: Import existing leads and tag aggressively.**
I dumped a CSV with sample contacts and added tags by source and matter type. The 10,000-contact limit on Tier 1 is the real guardrail to watch. For a solo shop, that is still a lot of runway.

**Step 4: Add two simple automations before anything advanced.**
Tier 1 includes 10 automation workflows and 2,000 workflow runs per month. I built one automation for instant acknowledgement after form submission, then a second automation for a 24-hour follow-up if no consult was booked.

**Step 5: Connect booking pages to the pipeline stage change.**
NinjaPipe includes 3 booking pages on Tier 1. I tied the booking confirmation to an automatic move from Qualified to Consultation Booked. This alone cuts a lot of manual dragging and status confusion.

**Step 6: Keep task and quote limits in view.**
Tier 1 includes 5,000 tasks and 5,000 quotes/invoices. I used tasks for consult prep and post-call follow-up reminders. If your practice runs very high admin volume, monitor those ceilings monthly.

From blank account to useful system took me roughly 95 minutes. Day two felt much better than day one because the flow started behaving like a system instead of a pile of apps.

## Honest Gotchas Before You Buy

NinjaPipe is broad. You can feel that during onboarding. If you want a tiny point solution that only schedules consults, this is bigger than you need.

Documentation and rollout pace can get out of sync. In recent user feedback, multiple buyers praised weekly updates, but also noted moments where new functionality moved faster than educational material. If your team needs hand-holding videos for every feature, budget extra setup time.

Support sentiment is mostly positive, but not perfectly uniform. I reviewed the most recent publicly visible AppSumo review pages and saw a recurring pattern: most reviewers praised fast founder response, while at least one reviewer reported delays and frustration waiting for help. That tells me support quality is strong overall, but you should still test response speed during your refund window.

One more practical limit: Tier 1 is capped at 1 workspace. If you want strict separation across brands or business units, this plan will feel tight quickly.

## ROI Math for a Solo Practice

Here is the simple math I used in testing. Assume you handle 12 new qualified inquiries each month. If each lead takes about 15 manual minutes for status updates, follow-up nudges, and booking coordination, that is 180 minutes, or 3 hours, burned on repetitive admin.

Add one more hour for missed follow-up recovery work and manual list cleanup, you are at 4 hours each month. For many solo attorneys billing around $250 per hour, that is about $1,000 in opportunity cost monthly.
