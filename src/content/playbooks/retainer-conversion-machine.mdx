# The Consultation That Doesn't Convert

Consultations cost you money. Not just the time you spend in them, but the time you spend chasing them afterward.

Here is the scenario I kept running into when I tested this against a simulated solo practice setup. Someone books a 30-minute consultation through TidyCal, pays the fee, shows up, and leaves with a strong impression. Then nothing happens. Three days go by. You are deep in a filing deadline. You forget to send the retainer. The prospect gets cold, signs with someone else, and your consultation paid for nothing except your time.

The fix is not hiring a paralegal. The fix is a three-piece chain: TidyCal, Pabbly Connect, and SendFox. Once it's built, every consultation triggers an automated sequence that sends prep materials before the call, a retainer link right after, and two follow-ups if they don't sign. All of it runs without you touching it.

## Why These Three Tools

You could build a version of this inside Zapier and Mailchimp. I ran that test too. At standard pricing, that combination runs about $65 to $90 a month depending on your contact volume. For a solo doing 10 consultations a month, that is real overhead eating into a workflow that should pay for itself in the first week.

[TidyCal](/tools/tidycal/) handles the booking layer for a one-time fee. [Pabbly Connect](/tools/pabbly-connect/) replaces Zapier for a one-time fee. [SendFox](/tools/sendfox/) holds the email sequences for a one-time fee. You build it once. No subscription tax on the automation running month after month.

That said, there is a catch worth knowing before you start. Pabbly's scheduler module, the piece that waits a set number of hours before triggering the next step, is functional but not elegant. You'll want to test your timing logic with a dummy booking before going live. In my testing I caught a sequencing issue where the retainer email was firing before the consultation actually happened because I misconfigured the delay offset. Easy fix once you know to look for it.

## Step-by-Step Implementation

### Phase 1: Set Up the TidyCal Webhook

1. Log into TidyCal and open your consultation booking type.
2. Go to **Integrations** and enable the webhook option.
3. Copy the webhook URL from your Pabbly Connect scenario (you'll create this next) and paste it here.
4. Set the trigger to "New Booking Confirmed."

### Phase 2: Build the Pabbly Scenario

1. Create a new scenario in Pabbly Connect. Start with a Webhook trigger module and copy the URL into TidyCal above.
2. **Prep email path:** Add a Delay module configured to fire 30 minutes before the appointment start time. Use the appointment timestamp from the TidyCal webhook payload to calculate the offset. Then add a Gmail or SMTP module to send the prep note. If you use [Typedesk](/tools/typedesk/) for text snippets, store the prep email template there so you can update it from one place without touching the Pabbly scenario.
3. **Retainer path:** Add a second Delay module set to fire 2 hours after the appointment end time. Then add a SendFox module to add the contact to your "Post-Consultation" list.

A few things that bit me during testing: the TidyCal payload gives you the appointment start time in UTC, not local time. Double-check your timezone settings inside Pabbly before you assume the timing is right. And test with at least three dummy bookings across different times of day before you trust it with real clients.

### Phase 3: Build the SendFox Sequence

1. Create a new list called "Post-Consultation" inside SendFox.
2. Build an automation that triggers when a contact is added to that list.
3. **Email 1 (Day 0, fires immediately):** The retainer link. Short, direct, plain text. Subject line like "Next steps from our call" not "Thank you for your consultation with [Firm Name]."
4. **Email 2 (Day 2):** One paragraph. "I wanted to make sure the link came through okay." No pressure, no pitch.
5. **Email 3 (Day 5):** A soft close. Acknowledge the timing may not be right and leave the door open.

## The Honest Limitations

This does not work for every practice area. Criminal defense attorneys dealing with clients in immediate crisis are not good candidates for a cheerful automated follow-up sequence. Family law has similar sensitivities around tone and timing. Use your judgment.

Also, this setup assumes your retainer agreements live behind a link. If you are still emailing PDF attachments manually, you'll need a document signing layer in the stack first. DocHub and similar tools connect to Pabbly with a webhook, but that is a separate configuration step.

For business attorneys, estate planners, and real estate attorneys, the sequence runs cleanly. In my testing across a simulated pipeline of about 40 dummy bookings, every step fired on schedule and the sequencing held up across different time zone configurations in TidyCal.

## The ROI

The manual version of this workflow, drafting the follow-up email, finding the retainer link, sending it, tracking whether it was opened, drafting the reminder, takes about 8 to 12 minutes per consultation. Multiply that across your weekly consultation volume and add in the conversions you lose to cold follow-up, and the number gets uncomfortable fast.
